Exploration and Analysis of Student Exam Performance¶

Arjun Patel, Navreet Bajwa, Yash Upadhyay¶

Introduction¶

Exam performance for students in high school is crucial to their future success. For our final project, we chose a dataset from Kaggle titled "Student Alcohol Consumption" which contains data gathered from a survey of students from two schools in Portugal about their weekday and weekend alcohol consumption as well as many other general attributes such as highest level of education achieved by their mother and father, how many extracurricular activities they're involved in, weekly study time, whether they want to pursue higher education, and many more.

The objective of our project was to try to predict exam performance using variables that seemed most intuitively relevant like study time, whether they take extra paid classes or not, whether or not they want to pursue higher education, and number of absences. We also investigated some of the more interesting and less intuitive variables like alcohol consumption, whether or not they're in a romantic relationship

Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA)/Data Visualization¶

The next step in the data analysis process is to perform some EDA to discover any relationships among the data we're interested in.

In order to get a feel for our data, we explored the linear correlation between all of our relevant variables. The correlation coefficient is a numerical value between -1 and 1, inclusive. It tells us the strength of the relationship between two variables, specifically a linear relationship in our case. Ideally, we want our correlation coefficient to be either greater than 0.5 or less than -0.5.

If you are interested in learning the details of correlation like how it's calculated and it's significance, please check out https://www.datascience.com/learn-data-science/fundamentals/introduction-to-correlation-python-data-science

We started by calculating the linear correlations using the built-in corr() function from our tidy_data dataframe. We then created a heatmap visualization of the correlation matrix using the Seaborn library. Each cell in the matrix contains the correlation coefficient between the corresponding row and column variable from our dataset.

From our heatmap visualization, we see that there are two main clusters where the correlations are closer to 1 or -1. The first cluster is the variables Medu and Fedu and it makes sense that these would be more strongly correlated. The level of education between the mother and father would usually be pretty similar. The other cluster is that around the G1 G2 and G3 scores and the Average Score we calculated. This also makes sense since Average Score itself is calculated using G1, G2 and G3. Overall, the analysis of linear correlations between the variables does not provide us with any major insights about our dataset.

We have quite a few variables which were categorical and had binary responses like: gender (F or M), parent cohabitation status (T for together or A for apart) etc. For these variables, we will conduct hypothesis tests to see if they are significant factors that contribute to average exam score. We will use a significance level of 0.10 rather than the standard 0.05 which increases our probability of a Type I error. To read more about this, please see https://machinelearningmastery.com/parametric-statistical-significance-tests-in-python/

We'll start with gender. First, we'll calculate the average exam score for the males and females to see if they are majorly different:

The average score for males is higher than for females but we can't say for sure if this is a significant difference without a formal hypothesis test. We then conduct a two-sample t test to see if there is a significant difference in average exam score between males and females for this population. Note that for this and the following categorical variables, we assume the variances are unequal for the two groups.

In [636]:
males = tidy_data.loc[tidy_data['sex'] == 0]
females = tidy_data.loc[tidy_data['sex'] == 1]
males = males.drop(['sex','age','Pstatus','failures','internet','famrel','Avg_Alc_Consumption',
    'studytime', 'paid', 'activities', 'romantic', 'freetime', 'goout', 'higher', 'absences', 'Parent_Education'],axis=1)
females = females.drop(['sex','age','Pstatus','failures','internet','famrel','Avg_Alc_Consumption',
    'studytime', 'paid', 'activities', 'romantic', 'freetime', 'goout', 'higher', 'absences', 'Parent_Education'],axis=1)
t,p = scipy.stats.ttest_ind(males, females, axis=0,equal_var=False) #assuming unequal variances between the groups
print("t-test p value is", float(p))

t-test p value is 0.045040833230012865

Our p-value of 0.045 < $ \alpha $ = 0.10 so we reject the null hypothesis that there is no significant difference in average score between males and females. Thus, we conclude that gender is a significant factor for predicting average score. When we later do our linear regression to try to predict average score, we will include gender as an independent variable.

Conclusions¶

The objective of our project was to investigate factors that may affect student exam performance. After going through the entire data analysis, we came to the conclusion that there is no one factor that best predicts exam performance. We started by looking at the correlations between the variables and student's exam scores. However, there were no strong correlations for any of the variables so we then conducted hypothesis tests for the variables to see if there was any significant effect on exam scores. We then applied machine learning by creating multiple linear regression models and then refining the model based on the p-values for each variable.

If we were to conduct further analysis, we could find other similar datasets and apply what we did here to see if any variables would be more substantial in predicting exam performance. We could also do more in-depth testing on each of the variables in relation to exam scores to try creating a better model.
